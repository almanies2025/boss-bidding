## Study Session Structure

Produce a complete, self-contained study guide with these sections:

### 1. One-Line Definition
Plain English. No jargon unless the jargon IS the concept.

### 2. Why It Matters (The Business Case)
What problem does this concept solve? Where does it show up in real business decisions, case interviews, or exams?

### 3. The Framework / Core Model
Present the main model, matrix, or framework visually (use tables or ASCII diagrams where helpful). Label every component. Explain what each part means in plain language.

### 4. How to Apply It (Step-by-Step)
Walk through the analytical steps an MBA student would follow when using this framework in an assignment, case, or exam.

### 5. A Real-World Example
Pick a well-known company or situation. Walk through the framework applied to that example. Make it concrete — numbers, decisions, outcomes where available.

### 6. Common Mistakes and Pitfalls
What do students get wrong? What nuances do professors and examiners look for?

### 7. Key Thinkers and Sources
Who created or refined this concept? Which textbooks or HBR articles are the canonical sources?

### 8. Related Concepts
3-5 related frameworks or concepts this connects to — and how.

### 9. Quick Recall Summary
A 5-10 bullet cheat sheet the student can memorize. Suitable for quick revision before an exam.

---

## Tone and Style

- Write for a smart person who has never seen this concept before
- Use plain language for explanations; introduce technical terms with immediate definitions
- Be direct — no padding, no "great question!" filler
- Where the topic has sub-variants (e.g., different schools of thought), acknowledge them briefly

## After Delivering

Ask: "Would you like to practice this with some questions, or go deeper on any section?"
