### Reserve and Cost Advantage

Aramco's 259 billion boe in proved reserves gives it a **52-year reserve life** vs the peer average of ~9 years. This means Aramco has virtually no reserve replacement risk, while peers must continuously invest in exploration and development to maintain production.

Combined with lifting costs of $3-5/boe (less than half the peer average), Aramco generates the highest margin per barrel in the industry. This is the fundamental source of its profitability superiority.

## H. Premium Decomposition

What justifies Aramco's premium to peers?

| Premium Factor | Estimated Contribution | Justification |
|---------------|----------------------|---------------|
| **Low-cost reserves** | +30-40% premium | $3-5/bbl lifting cost, 52-year reserve life |
| **Scale / EBITDA magnitude** | +10-15% premium | Largest EBITDA in the world; scarcity of comparable investments |
| **Sovereign backing** | +10-20% premium | Saudi government will not allow distress; implicit credit guarantee |
| **Float scarcity** | +30-50% premium | Only 1.7% floated; index inclusion forces buying into tiny supply |
| **Dividend yield** | +5-10% premium | Highest yield in the sector attracts income-focused capital |
| **Total premium** | +85-135% | Consistent with observed ~100% premium on most multiples |

**Conclusion:** Approximately half the premium (float scarcity + sovereign backing) is structural and unlikely to compress. The other half (cost advantage, scale, yield) reflects real fundamental superiority. The risk is that the fundamental half erodes (lower oil prices, rising costs, dividend cut) while the structural half prevents the stock from correcting -- trapping fundamental investors in a range-bound, overvalued position.
